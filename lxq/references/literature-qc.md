## Review question and protocol

Define intended use and review type before searching: rapid background scan, scoping review, systematic review support, citation verification, methods reconstruction, grant landscape, or paper-specific deep reading. Frame the question with the relevant population/system, exposure/intervention, comparator, outcome, study design, date/language limits, and exclusions.

Record databases, interfaces, search dates, exact queries, filters, deduplication rules, screening stages, and reasons for exclusion. Do not label an informal search “systematic.” Route formal systematic-review work to the applicable reporting standard and qualified information specialist when needed.

## Search and selection

- Prefer primary structured sources appropriate to the field, such as PubMed, Crossref, arXiv/bioRxiv/medRxiv, and official publisher or repository records.
- Use secondary discovery tools to expand candidates, not as the sole metadata or evidence authority.
- Resolve DOI, PMID, PMCID, arXiv ID, title, authors, year, journal, article type, version, correction/retraction status, and duplicate relationships.
- Preserve inclusion/exclusion decisions and distinguish title/abstract screening from full-text assessment.
- Treat citation count and journal prestige as context, not proof of support or quality.

Route coordinated discovery, MeSH strategy, citation verification, deduplication, and RIS/BibTeX management to `nature-academic-search` when available.

## Lawful full-text acquisition

Acquire only through lawful and authorized routes:

1. user-provided or institutionally authorized copy;
2. publisher open-access article;
3. PubMed Central or another recognized repository;
4. arXiv, bioRxiv, medRxiv, or author-accepted manuscript in an institutional repository;
5. library link resolver or document-delivery route available to the user.

Do not bypass paywalls, access controls, CAPTCHA, robots restrictions, licenses, or authentication. Do not use pirated repositories. If full text is unavailable, retain verified metadata/abstract, label `full_text_unavailable`, and request a user-authorized copy.

For every obtained file record: stable paper ID, DOI/PMID/PMCID/arXiv ID, source URL, access route, license/status if visible, retrieved date, version, filename, file size, SHA-256, and identity verification. Verify that PDF title, authors, DOI and version match the selected record; an HTML error page renamed `.pdf` is not a paper.

## Paper reading and critical appraisal

Identify article type and build a stable source map before summarizing. Capture exact source anchors for claims, methods, results, limitations, figures, tables, supplements, data/code statements, corrections, and uncertainty.

For each paper distinguish:

- research question and design;
- population/model, sampling, controls, experimental unit, and exclusions;
- intervention/exposure, outcomes, contrasts, analysis, multiplicity, and missingness;
- main effect sizes and uncertainty, not P values alone;
- figure/table evidence and whether prose matches it;
- exploratory versus confirmatory findings;
- internal validity, bias, confounding, leakage, external validity, and reproducibility;
- author claims, directly supported conclusions, plausible interpretations, and unsupported extrapolations.

Route full bilingual, figure/table-aware reading to `nature-reader`. Do not replace a requested full-paper reader with a summary. For copyrighted sources, keep long reproductions in lawful local artifacts and keep chat quotations brief.

## Cross-paper evidence synthesis

Use a study evidence matrix with stable IDs. Group papers by question and design rather than narrating one paper at a time. Reconcile population, assay, endpoint, comparator, effect direction, uncertainty, bias, and applicability before combining conclusions.

Grade claim support as `strong`, `partial`, `background`, `contradictory-or-limiting`, `metadata-only`, or `not-assessable`. Never cite a metadata-only candidate as substantive support. Report heterogeneity and negative or contradictory studies instead of forcing consensus.

For quantitative synthesis, do not pool studies until effect definitions, units, study designs, independence, and required statistics are compatible. Route formal meta-analysis to a dedicated statistical protocol and retain the extraction/audit trail.
